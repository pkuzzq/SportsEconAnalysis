How You See It

Dueling answers? No, different questions

We all know the “room full of economists” joke. But having personally been in many a room full of economists, the joke is an indictment of the teller, not the economist. If you ask a room full of economists the same economic question, you will get the same answer. If you ask them different questions, you will, indeed, get different answers.

And so we come to the recent SportsBusiness Journal article, “Would stadium pay off for city? Dueling studies reach different conclusions on proposed Cowboys stadium” (Nov. 1-7 issue).

The article lists three economic impact studies on the issue. One by Economic Research Associates (ERA) arrives at $238 million in annual economic impact for Arlington, Texas. Another study by Turnkey Sports/SportsEconomics says the impact is $51 million annually for the current Cowboys hosts in Irving, Texas. A third study by well-known academic analysts Mark Rosentraub and David Swindell (the R-S study) suggests that Arlington would lose $250 million over 30 years under the tentative arrangement between the city and the Cowboys’ team owner.

Now, any thoughtful person might wonder about this variation. That thoughtful person could call a sports economist like me and ask. Having read the reports, I would have responded: Each study finds a different number because each addresses a different question. ERA reports total spending that residents of Arlington can hope will be diverted from Irving. Turnkey reports the extra spending that happens in Irving just because the Cowboys are there. Finally, R-S looks at the ongoing dollar subsidy that Arlington taxpayers should expect after they’ve lured the Cowboys with a new stadium.

Far from the “dueling studies” portrayed in the title of the article, all three studies generate important and different information about the spending issue.

But the article doesn’t follow the thoughtful path. Instead, it asks three sports management/marketing people their opinion of this variation in estimates. Apparently, since the estimates are of economic impact, SportsBusiness Journal and its (non-economist) experts assume economists produced the estimates (without checking — neither Rosentraub nor Swindell are economists).

Then follows an attack on the integrity of the analysts behind the estimates: “So who’s right and who’s wrong? There’s no definitive answer; unless you’re the expert hired to produce the results that reflect the interests of your client, according to three college educators who teach in economics and sports management.” (A quick Internet search revealed that none of these three teaches in economics; all teach in sports industry programs.)

So, rather than recognize that three different questions were asked (and one of them had to do with Irving rather than Arlington), SportsBusiness Journal brands the analysts behind the reports as money-grubbing yes-men bent only on twisting data to substantiate the claims of their sugar daddy clients.

It’s a dismaying personal attack on people of high reputation; a dismaying distraction of the attention of policy-makers away from the careful assessment of the costs and benefits of their choices; a dismaying and groundless tainting of a talented group of people who make insightful contributions to the policy process.

By the way, the article missed one. It mentions that Las Colinas was considered as another alternative location by the Cowboys owner, but fails to report that an economic impact assessment was done there. According to the Star-Telegram, economists at the University of North Texas found that the Cowboys and their stadium were worth $346 million annually to Dallas County.

In the final analysis, the process can only be better with many questions addressed. This additional study addresses yet a further question — what are the team and stadium worth to the entire county? Here’s hoping that, eventually, the value of asking and answering as many questions as possible is recognized rather than scorned.

Rodney Fort
Pullman, Wash.
